1884
First underground subway plans were approved in order to combat weather conditions.

Although plans for an underground subway were initially made in 1869 by Alfredy Ely Beach, it would take until 1884 for things to be approved after New Yorkers realized the benefits of an underground system.

1904
Subway plans finish and open to the public.
Learn more...

The subway first opens at City Hall station with hundreds of New Yorkers paying 4 cents to view the inaugural ride. The subway would be leased to the IRT (Interborough Rapid Transit company), a name still known by older New Yorkers to this day. This subway line would run from City Hall to 145th Street and would be referred to as "City Hall to Harlem in 15 Minutes" allowing New Yorkers to commute and explore further out of the city.

1908
Brooklyn-Manhattan Transit (BMT) enters the game.
Learn more...

Another privately owned company enters the subway system, taking over different lines from the IRT. As seen in the name, BMT operated mainly in Brookyln and Manhattan whereas the IRT operated in Manhattan, going to the Bronx. In today's terms, the IRT would be the numbered lines whereas the BMT would be the lettered lines.

1932
The City tries its hand at subways.
Learn more...

Wanting to eventually unify the subway under one name, the city launched the Independent Subway System (IND) in 1932. The IND was made with full intent to compete and hopefully run the other two companies out of business in order to take over and unite the subway systems.

1940
IND, BMT and IRT unite.
Learn more...

By 1940, the IND successfully bought out the privately owned BMT and IRT for $317 million, succeeding in gaining access to all the subway systems throughout the city. Several BMT and IND routes were connected due to them sharing similar route designs regarding stations and tunnels. This would be known as the B division. The IRT remained alone and separated in the A division due to their smaller stations, narrower tunnels and sharper curves that were unable to house the B division rolling stock. In order to connect both divisions, stations were instead connected by having passengers walk from a route to another without having to pay another fare similar to how Times Square has most of the city's trains within walking distance underground.

1953
NYCTA created, takes over NYC transportation.
Learn more...

The New York City Transit Authority (NYCTA) was created in 1953 in order to manage transportation in the city, ranging from buses, subways and streetcars. They would eventually be placed under state-level control as the Metropolitan Transportation Authority or (MTA).

1970s-80s
Subway transportation hits a lull with low ridership and crime rampant.
Learn more...

In the 1970s-1980s, the NYC subway struggled to maintain high ridership and keep its crime and vandalism low. Delays and poor maintenance also contributed to passengers seeking out other transportation options. Hundreds of felonies were committed on the subway system each week during the 1980s. However, the NYCTA pressed on under lead of David Gunn, president of the NYCTA, opening several new subway stations, ordering hundreds of new subway cars and cleaned up the graffiti-ridden rolling stock. NYC's police force joined as well in order to combat crime and make the subway a safe place again. Going into the 1990s, ridership began to rise and subway conditions began to climb once again.

1993
The MetroCard is born!
Learn more...

Ridership was booming once again and in order to capitalize, the MTA introduced the MetroCard. With the goal of making fare collection automated and less tedious, stations everywhere were updated with MetroCard compatible turnstiles, speeding up the entrance process and cutting costs due to tokens costing more to operate with. Tokens were eventually fully phased out by 2003.

1990s-2000s
Modernization abound, helping to speed up service and enhance the passenger experience.
Learn more...

In the 1990s, the MTA began to experiment with communications-based train control (CBTC), a system that allowed more trains to operate on the tracks by improving communication. Although initially implemented only on the BMT Canarsie line, it has been added to several other lines through the next two decades. The MTA also started to add digital displays on the train stations to display when the trains would arrive as well as help stations for assistance and emergencies. In 2011, Travel Station kiosks were installed giving city natives and tourists alike a one stop shop for information on the subway, navigation and city information.

2020+
What's next?
Learn more...

The MTA continues to improve the system and enhance passenger experience even if there are still numerous problems with the subway. Metrocards are slated to be phased out by 2024, replaced by the widely used OMNY system where passengers can simply tap their credit cards for access. All subway lines should be updated with CBTC as well as newer rolling stock with better features such as open gangways, digital displays and lighted doorways. Ridership is still heavily decreased in the wake of COVID-19 but is slowly climbing back up. We will just have to see how the MTA can continue to work on and hopefully improve the subway system for the millions of New Yorkers that ride it daily.
